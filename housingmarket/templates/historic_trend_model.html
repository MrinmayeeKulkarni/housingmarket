Historical Trend Based Predictive Model

Goal

Given the historical housing prices dataset, our attempt was to predict the future housing prices trend for the two urban regions – Seattle and DMV. In order to accomplish this, we applied Machine Learning techniques on our dataset.

Dataset

Acknowledgement: Zillow – Economic Data - Home Values Median Home Value Per Sq Ft ($) Per County

Data has median estimated home value per square feet across two urban regions – Seattle and DMV. The data was granular at the county level. The original dataset we acquired from Zillow has data since 1992. To ensure the highest quality and cleanliness, we chose to utilize the data points since 2000.

To summarize, the data set included categorical features like “State” and “County”; with “Housing Price Per SqFt” and “Year” being the only numerical elements.

Approach

Model

As a first step in designing a predictive model we chose the Simple Linear regression model to find if there is a linear relationship between the Housing Prices and Year over Year trend of the geographical boundaries.

Scikit-Learn Python packages are used to create, train and evaluate the model. Pandas and Matplotlib libraries are used to load, process and visualize the datasets.

Why Linear Regression?

We have a dataset which contains the continuous variables that a Linear Regression would use to find a statistical relationship.

Alternative models

Other models such as logistic regression, decision tree etc. were considered. However, due to the limited set of features available in this dataset we could not use an alternative model.

Methodology

Data Pre-processing

  • Data Split for Training and Testing – our dataset was split into two equal parts based on the year. Data from 2000 – 2010 was used for training and the other remaining was used for testing the accuracy of the model.

  • Removal of outliers and non-numeric values – as we started analyzing our dataset, we observed some outliers in prices and also some missing values. This warranted a cleanup of the data prior to feeding it to the model for training. We made an attempt to substitute the missing data using linear interpolation technique. Quantiles of 0.25 - 0.75 were used to get the lower and upper bounds for removing outliers.

  • (dis)similaraties between train and test datasets – after dividing the original dataset into train and test parts, there were dissimilarities in the shapes. Not all Counties that were present in the training dataset were available in testing dataset and vice versa. To rectify this, we reshaped our datasets to match by augmenting/removing the mismatched county data.


Model Training, Testing, Tuning

Our historical housing prices data was fed to the Scikit-Learn’s linear regression model for training. We then tested the model using the training the dataset. We also calculated the residuals and coefficients which would help determine the accuracy of the model.

Conclusion

Met the goal?

Given the scope of the dataset, although a 100% accuracy is not achieved, the model was able to give a reasonable prediction that shows a linear trend based on the years 2000 - 2010 .

What could have helped for this model?

Bringing in more dependent and independent variables such as year built, technology company growth, employment rate, occupational inflow to urban cities etc. Training with more extensive, richer in terms of years, as well as larger volume of data. Including external factors such as Dollar($) value inflation, Overall GDP rate etc.
